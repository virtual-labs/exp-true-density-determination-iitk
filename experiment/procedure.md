<b>Materials Required:</b><br>
● Pycnometer or specific gravity bottle (25 mL)<br>
● Analytical balance<br>
● Solvent (e.g., water, ethanol, or other liquid in which the powder is insoluble)<br>
● Powder sample (e.g., zinc oxide, starch, or talc)<br>
● Funnel<br>
● Beaker<br>
● Glass rod<br>
● Vacuum pump (optional, to remove air bubbles)<br>

<b>Procedure:</b><br>
● Clean and dry the specific gravity bottle (25 mL) and place the stopper.<br>
● Weigh the empty bottle and stopper — record the weight as W₁.<br>
● Fill the bottle completely with water (or the selected liquid) and weigh — record as W₂.<br>
● Remove the liquid and dry the bottle thoroughly.<br>
● Add about 5 g of the powder sample into the bottle (for heavy powders) or 1–2 g (for light powders). Weigh the bottle and powder — record as W₃.<br>
● Slowly add the liquid to the bottle containing powder while gently swirling to avoid air entrapment. Use a vacuum pump if needed. Fill up to the mark and weigh — record as W₄.<br>
● Process the recorded data as described below to calculate the true density.<br>

<b>Precautions:</b><br>
●  Ensure that the powder is insoluble in the selected solvent.<br>
●  Avoid air bubble entrapment during addition of liquid.<br>
●  Maintain constant temperature throughout the experiment for accuracy.<br>
●  Handle the pycnometer carefully to avoid errors due to weight fluctuations.<br>

<b>Conclusion:</b><br>
The solvent displacement method provides a reliable means to determine the true density of powders. The use of a pycnometer minimizes voids and enhances accuracy. True density helps in evaluating powder properties like porosity, compressibility, and flow behavior in pharmaceutical formulations.
